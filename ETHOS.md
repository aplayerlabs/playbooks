# Ethos

## The problem

Every business has bottlenecks that software could solve. Most business owners can't build software. So they either hire someone expensive, use a no-code tool that doesn't quite fit, or live with the bottleneck.

AI changed the cost of building software. It didn't change the cost of thinking clearly about what to build. That's the harder problem.

The real bottleneck isn't code — it's the invisible expertise trapped in the business owner's head. The pattern recognition, the judgment calls, the shortcuts they can't articulate. That tacit knowledge is the most valuable asset in the business and the hardest thing to extract. A Player Brains is a system for getting it out and turning it into software.

## What we believe

**Problem-first, always.** Ideas are cheap. Problems are real. Every project starts by validating that a real problem exists for a real person. If it doesn't, we say so. Building the wrong thing fast is worse than not building at all.

**Thinking before building.** The most expensive mistake in software is building something nobody needs. Before a single line of code is written, you'll stress-test the direction: what could kill this, what guarantees failure, where's the real constraint, which approach keeps the most doors open. This takes one session. It saves weeks.

**A brain is not a prompt, it's a system.** Prompts fill in blanks. Brains think. Each brain in A Player Brains has structure, constraints, heuristics, guardrails, and refusal conditions. It knows when to push back and when to keep moving. This is what separates a brain from every "prompt library" on the internet — a prompt is a question, a brain is an operator.

**Guard rails, not blank canvases.** A blank canvas is terrifying if you don't know what you're doing. A Player Brains is opinionated. It chooses the framework. It chooses the hosting. It chooses the database. You make the product decisions — what it does, who it's for, what it looks like. The brains handle the how.

**Train tracks, not choose-your-own-adventure.** The brain chain is linear. One brain at a time, in order. You can't accidentally skip a step. You can't get lost. If you need to go back, /wrap captures your state and points you to the right brain. The track keeps you moving.

**Plain English, always.** Every brain produces a summary you can read without technical knowledge. STATUS.md tells you where you are, what's done, and what's next — in sentences, not code. The technical artifacts exist underneath for the brains to coordinate. You never need to read them.

**Every brain stands alone.** The brain chain is the recommended path. But if you know what you need, you can call any brain directly. /test works without /build. /design works without /define. The brains are smart enough to figure out what's missing and tell you.

**First pancake mindset.** v1 will be clunky. That's iteration, not failure. If the first version is 50% better than doing it manually, that's already a win. The skill of building brains compounds — by the tenth brain you're cranking them out. Set expectations accordingly: the first one teaches you, the second one is faster, the fifth one is second nature.

**Performance over throughput.** Brains create capacity, not volume. The goal isn't "do more" — it's "do better with less drag." If you fill the freed capacity with more busy work, you've missed the point. The leverage is in doing fewer things at a higher level, not in doing the same things faster.

**Ship, don't perfect.** The brain chain ends at /launch — your app, live on the internet. Not "almost done." Not "works on my machine." Live. The goal is always a working application in the hands of real users.

## Who this is not for

If you're a developer, you don't need guard rails — you need sharper tools. Look at [gstack](https://github.com/garrytan/gstack) by Garry Tan.

If you want to learn to code, this isn't a teaching tool. It's a building tool. You'll ship software without understanding how it works underneath. That's the point.

If you want pixel-perfect custom design, the /design brain will get you far, but it's not a replacement for a design team on a complex product.

## Who this is for

You run a business. You have a problem that software could solve. You want it solved — not explained, not spec'd, not estimated. Solved. A Player Brains is the brain chain that gets you from "I have this problem" to "here's the app that fixes it."

That's it. That's the whole thing.
